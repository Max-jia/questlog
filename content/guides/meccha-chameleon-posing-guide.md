### Why Posing Matters More Than Painting

![Why Posing Matters More Than Painting](/images/meccha-chameleon/screenshot-09.jpg)


Your human brain is hardwired to recognize human shapes. When a seeker scans a room, their brain processes **silhouettes before colors**. A perfect paint job with a human-shaped outline will always be caught faster than a mediocre paint job with a broken silhouette.

Posing is not optional, it's the #1 factor in whether you survive.

---

### How the Pose System Works

Press **R** to open the pose wheel. Hover over a pose and click to lock it in. You can change poses as many times as you want during prep phase. Once locked, your character holds that pose for the entire hunt phase (unless you move).

---

### Every Pose, When to Use It

### Starfish (Lie Flat)
**What it looks like:** Arms and legs spread, body completely flat.

**Best for:**
- Flat walls (like a poster or wall decoration)
- Ceilings (you become part of the ceiling texture)
- Floors (you look like a rug or floor pattern)
- Large, empty surfaces where standing or crouching would create an obvious bump

**Avoid:** Curved surfaces. The flat pose won't follow the curve and will float off the surface.

---

### Standing Upright
**What it looks like:** Standing straight, arms at sides.

**Best for:**
- Curved objects (barrels, tree trunks, columns)
- Tall narrow spaces (between bookshelves, behind curtains)
- Mimicking standing objects (statues, signposts, mannequins)

**Avoid:** Flat walls, the standing pose creates too much depth and is instantly recognizable as human.

---

### Crouch / Ball Curl
**What it looks like:** Crouched low, curled into a compact shape.

**Best for:**
- Small objects (pumpkins, balls, crates, boulders)
- Tight spaces (under tables, inside lockers, between boxes)
- Vegetable patches, candy piles, and other clusters of round objects

**Avoid:** Large flat surfaces, the curled shape makes no sense on a flat wall.

---

### Sitting / Leaning
**What it looks like:** Sitting on the ground or leaning against a surface.

**Best for:**
- Mimicking furniture (chairs, benches)
- Leaning against other objects to blend in as part of a scene
- Themed hiding spots (sitting among other "seated" decorations)

**Avoid as a beginner:** These are the hardest poses to pull off convincingly. Master the other poses first.

---

### The ESC Menu Trick (Advanced)

Here's a mechanic most players don't know about:

1. Lock in a pose with **R**.
2. Press **ESC** to open the pause menu.
3. While the menu is open, you can **move your upper body independently** using the mouse.

**What this lets you do:**
- Twist your torso while your legs stay in pose
- Arch your back to match curved surfaces
- Create genuinely inhuman shapes that seekers' brains can't pattern-match

This trick works because the game stops rendering your character normally when the menu is open, allowing independent limb movement. When you close the menu, the new position is saved.

### Matching Poses to Your Hiding Spot, Flowchart

```
What shape is your hiding surface?
│
├── Flat wall/ceiling → Starfish pose
├── Curved object (barrel, column) → Standing upright
├── Small round object cluster → Ball curl
├── Tight space (under/inside something) → Ball curl
├── Among furniture → Sitting/leaning (advanced)
└── Next to other standing objects → Standing upright
```

### The Most Common Posing Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---------|-------------|
| Painting first, posing second | The paint might warp when you change pose. Always **pose first, then paint**. |
| Using the same pose every round | Seekers learn individual playstyles. Rotate your pose choices. |
| Standing upright on flat walls | The human outline is unmistakable. Lie flat instead. |
| Posing too late in prep phase | You need time to check all angles after locking the pose. |
| Not checking from the seeker's perspective | Use free cam (4) to view yourself from ground level before the hunt starts. |
